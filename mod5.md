# Module 5: International Trade

## Introduction to International Trade
*   International trade plays a dominant role in the economic development of a country by helping it widen its market.  
*   International trade refers to the exchange of capital, goods, and services across international borders.  
*   Without trade, nations would have to rely only on products produced domestically.  

---

## Advantages and Disadvantages of International Trade

### Advantages
Engaging in international trade offers several significant benefits to participating nations:  
*   Optimal use of natural resources.  
*   Availability of all types of goods.  
*   Advantages of large-scale production.  
*   Stability in prices.  
*   Exchange of technical know-how and establishment of new industries.  
*   Development of the means of transport and communication.  
*   Ability to face natural calamities.  
*   International cooperation and understanding.  

### Disadvantages
Despite its benefits, international trade can also have negative impacts:  
*   Impediment in the development of home industries.  
*   Economic dependence on other nations.  
*   Political dependence.  
*   Import of harmful goods.  
*   Free trade can lead to pollution and other environmental problems.  
*   It is considered unwise to export all the natural resources of a country.  

---

## Adam Smith's Theory of Absolute Advantage
The concept of absolute advantage was developed by Adam Smith in his foundational work, *The Wealth of Nations*.  

### Assumptions of the Theory
The theory is based on several key assumptions:  
*   Labour is the only relevant factor of production.  
*   There are no barriers to trade in goods.  
*   Production exhibits constant returns to scale.  
*   There are no transportation costs.  
*   Labour is mobile within a country but immobile between countries.  

### Statement of Theory
*   Countries should specialize in producing the goods and services in which they have an absolute advantage and engage in free trade with other countries to sell their goods.  
*   By doing so, a country's resources would be utilized in the best possible way—specifically, in the production of goods and services where the country has a productivity advantage compared to others—and national wealth would be maximized.  

### Example of Absolute Advantage
The following table provides the man-hours required to produce a unit of wheat and cloth in the U.S. and the U.K.:  

| Product | U.S. | U.K. |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Wheat** | 3 | 10 |
| **Cloth** | 6 | 4 |

> **Note:** The data above represents the labour cost in hours required to produce one unit of wheat or cloth. 

#### Analysis of the Table:
*   To produce one unit of wheat, **3 hours** of labour are required in the U.S. and **10 hours** in the U.K.  
*   To produce one unit of cloth, **6 hours** of labour are required in the U.S. and **4 hours** in the U.K.  
*   **Therefore**, the U.S. can produce wheat more efficiently (at a lower cost), while the U.K. can produce cloth more efficiently.  

### Criticisms of the Theory
Smith's theory faces several criticisms in practical application:  
*   The theory assumes that each exporting country has an absolute advantage in the production of a commodity.  
*   Developing countries may not have any such absolute advantage.  
*   There are a large number of factors influencing trade between countries beyond just labour efficiency.  
*   The theory does not account for the fact that countries are often forced to export in order to neutralize their balance of payments deficit.

## Comparative Advantage Theory
Developed by David Ricardo.   

### Statement of Theory
*   According to Ricardo, a country can still gain from international trade even if it does not have an absolute advantage in producing both commodities.   
*   In this situation, the country should specialize in producing and exporting the commodity where its absolute disadvantage is smaller.   
*   Conversely, the country should import the commodity where its absolute disadvantage is greater.   

### Assumptions
*   Labour is the only relevant factor of production.   
*   Production exhibits constant returns to scale.   
*   There are no transportation costs.   
*   Labour is mobile within a country but immobile between countries.   
*   There is no intervention by the government in the economic system.   
*   Perfect competition exists both in the commodity and factor markets.   
*   There is full employment of resources in both countries.   

### Example of Labour Costs
The theory uses an example analyzing the labour cost in hours required to produce one unit of wheat or cloth.   

*   **U.S.:** Requires **3 hours** to produce wheat and **4 hours** to produce cloth.   
*   **U.K.:** Requires **10 hours** to produce wheat and **6 hours** to produce cloth.   

*Note on the analysis:*
*   The data indicates that England has an absolute advantage in producing both commodities through smaller inputs of labour than the UK. *(Note: transcribed exactly from provided notes, though it likely meant the U.S. has the absolute advantage over the U.K.)*
*   However, this does not mean that England will specialize in both cloth and wheat while the UK has nothing to export.   
*   England has a comparative cost advantage in producing wheat, and the UK has a comparatively lesser cost disadvantage in producing cloth.   
*   Accordingly, the US will specialize in producing and exporting wheat, while the UK will specialize in producing and exporting cloth.   

### Criticism
*   The theory assumes that there are no other costs except labour costs.   
*   The theory assumes constant returns to scale, but diminishing returns are likely to set in as the scale of production increases.   
*   The theory ignores differences in transport costs.   
*   The assumption that labour is mobile only within the country is not valid.   
*   Actual international trade is influenced by various government restrictions, like tariffs and other trade restrictions.   
*   The assumption of full employment is not valid.

## Heckscher - Ohlin Theory of International Trade
This theory is also known as the **Factor Endowment Theory**.   
*   The theory was originally developed by Eli Heckscher in 1919 and was later refined by Bertil Ohlin in 1935.   
*   As a result, it is commonly known as the Heckscher-Ohlin (H-O) Theorem.   

### Statement of Theory
*   The Heckscher-Ohlin Theorem states that a country will produce and export the commodity whose production requires the intensive use of the nation's relatively abundant and cheap factor.   
*   Additionally, the country will import the commodity whose production requires the intense use of a relatively scarce and expensive factor.   
*   In other words, a relatively labor-abundant country will export the relatively labor-intensive commodity and import the relatively capital-intensive commodity.   

### Assumptions
*   There are only two factors of production.   
*   It is a two-country, two-commodity model.   
*   There is perfect competition.   
*   There is full employment.   
*   There are no transport costs.   

### Merits
*   The H-O theory takes into account both demand and supply factors when determining international trade.   
*   This model lays down a permanent basis for international trade.   
*   The theory accurately maintains that production involves two key factors of production: labour and capital.   

### Criticism
*   It relies on a limited two-commodity model.   
*   The theory assumes perfect competition.   
*   The theory assumes that there is full employment.   
*   The theory ignores differences in transport costs.

# Balance of Payments (BoP) Overview
*   The balance of payments summarises the economic transactions of an economy with the rest of the world.  
*   These transactions include exports and imports of goods, services, and financial assets, along with transfer payments like foreign aid.  
*   The BOP statement acts as an indicator of a country's economic status in the world.  
*   It is a crucial tool that helps the government formulate its trade policies.  

---

## Components of Balance of Payments
The BOP is divided into three main components: the current account, the capital account, and the financial account.  

### 1. The Current Account (Goods & Services)
This account is used to monitor the inflow and outflow of goods and services in a country. It is termed "current" because it refers to transactions that are currently occurring and do not give rise to future claims.  
*   **Merchandise trade:** Also known as visible trade, this involves the trade in manufactured goods and raw materials.  
*   **Services:** Also known as invisible trade, this involves the sale of services such as travel, tourism, insurance, engineering, patents, and copyrights.  
*   **Income receipts:** This includes income derived from the ownership of assets abroad, such as dividends from shares and interest from bonds.  
*   **Unilateral transfers:** These consist of worker remittances from abroad, foreign aid, contributions to charitable institutions, and gifts between countries.  

### 2. The Capital Account (Asset)
The capital account refers to the net change in ownership of foreign assets. It serves as the record for all international capital transfers.  
*   It includes capital transfers.  
*   It also includes the purchase and sale of real assets.  

### 3. The Financial Account (Investments & Intangibles)
This account documents international monetary flows related to investment in business, real estate, bonds, and stocks.  
*   It includes government-owned assets such as foreign reserves, gold, and special drawing rights held with the International Monetary Fund.  
*   It also tracks private assets held abroad and direct foreign investment.  

### Other BOP Components
*   **Official reserve account:** This refers to the foreign currency held by a country's central bank, which is used to balance the payments from year to year.  
*   **Errors and Omissions:** This entry is made to reflect imperfections or errors that creep in during the compilation of the BOP statement, causing it not to balance.  

---

## BOP Disequilibrium: Deficit and Surplus
*   A disequilibrium in the balance of payments means it is in a condition of either a surplus or a deficit.  
*   A surplus occurs when Total Receipts exceed Total Payments, meaning **CREDIT > DEBIT**.  
*   A deficit occurs when Total Payments exceed Total Receipts, meaning **CREDIT < DEBIT**.  

---

## Reasons for BOP Disequilibrium

### Economic Factors
*   High levels of imports in a country will lead to BOP deficits.  
*   A lack of adequate international capital inflow into a country causes disequilibrium.  
*   External borrowings and loans from foreign countries also contribute to imbalances.  

### Political Factors
*   Political instability creates uncertainty among foreign investors, leading to a reduced inflow of foreign capital.  
*   The fear of war with another country can also trigger a disequilibrium in the BOP.  

### Structural Factors
*   Providing a high degree of protection to domestic industries leads to inefficiency and poor-quality products, which causes exports to suffer.  

### Social Factors
*   Countries like India heavily export agriculture and agro-based products, the prices of which fluctuate heavily in world markets.  
*   Agricultural exports are also constantly vulnerable to crop failures.  

### Technological Factors
*   A lack of thrust in research and development leaves countries with very few products that foreigners find attractive.  
*   Excessive stress on technology-intensive, export-oriented industries by competing countries (like China) results in BOP disequilibrium for other nations.  

---

## Methods to Correct BOP Disequilibrium

### Monetary Measures
*   **Monetary Policy:** A reduction in the money supply decreases the purchasing power of the people. This decline in demand brings prices down, which reduces imports and encourages exports.  
*   **Devaluation:** This is the lowering of the exchange value of a country's currency. Devaluation makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, thereby reducing the BOP deficit.  

### Trade Policy Measures
*   **Export Promotion:** Countries can adopt measures like reducing export duties, providing incentives and subsidies to exporters, and exempting exports from taxes.  
*   **Import Substitution:** Steps are taken to encourage the domestic production of goods that are currently being imported. This substitution helps save valuable foreign exchange.  

### Non-Monetary Measures
*   Promoting international travel and tourism.  
*   Inviting foreign companies to invest in the country.  
*   Ensuring citizens working abroad deposit their savings in domestic banks.  
*   Postponing debt repayments.  
*   Keeping inflation under control.  
*   Implementing checks on smuggling activities.

# Unit Notes: International Economics & Trade Policy

## 1. Devaluation
*   **Definition:** It is the lowering of the exchange value of the currency of a country.  
*   **Mechanism:** When a country devalues its currency, exports become cheaper and imports become expensive which causes a reduction in the BOP (Balance of Payments) deficit.  

### Objectives of Devaluation
*   To boost exports.  
*   Imports become more expensive and exports become more competitive and lucrative.  
*   To encourage a greater quantity of export from the country whose currency is being devalued.  
*   To reduce trade deficits. The increase in exports along with a reduction in imports will cause a positive impact on the balance of trade.  
*   To lower the cost of a country's external debt.  

### Limitations of Devaluation
*   Devaluation may cause inflation. Higher exports due to the devaluation in the currency will increase the level of income of the consumers causing domestic demand to rise, which raises prices.  
*   It can result in an increase in the production cost of commodities that depend on inputs that are imported.  
*   Domestic companies that have taken international loans will face greater servicing costs.  
*   It will foster uncertainty within the global markets.  
*   Devaluation may also spark trade wars. It will create tension with other competing countries.  

---

## 2. Free Trade vs. Protectionism

### A. Free Trade
*   **Definition:** Free trade occurs when there are no artificial barriers put in place by governments to restrict the flow of goods and services between trading nations.  

> **Note on Barriers:** When trade barriers, such as tariffs and subsidies are put in place, they protect domestic producers from international competition and redirect, rather than create trade flows.  

#### Advantages of Free Trade
*   International trade enables countries to specialize in the production of those commodities in which they have a comparative advantage.  
*   It increases the size of a firm's market, resulting in lower average costs and higher productivity.  
*   International competition promotes innovative production, new technology and marketing.  
*   Consumers benefit in the domestic economy as they can now obtain a greater variety of goods and services.  
*   The increased competition ensures commodities are supplied at the lowest prices.  

### B. Protectionism
*   **Definition:** It is the policy of protecting domestic industries by imposing high customs duties on foreign products.  
*   **Theoretical Stance:** Economic theory does not rule out protectionism as a welfare maximizing policy option.  

#### Advantages of Protectionism
*   **The Infant Industry Argument:** This is the main argument for protectionism. Industries in developing countries can effectively compete with those already developed if they receive some initial protection in the form of tariffs or subsidies.  
*   **Increased Economic Stability:** Economies do not become dependent on global markets. This means that businesses are not vulnerable to downturns in the economies of their trading partners.  
    > **Example:** A recession in the USA leads to decreased demand for India's exports, leading to falling export incomes, lower GDP, lower incomes, lower domestic demand, and rising unemployment.  
*   **Protection Against Dumping:** Countries with surplus products may dump them on world markets at prices below the cost of production. Protectionism guards against this.  
*   **Correcting Unfavourable Terms of Trade:** Countries whose economies are largely agricultural face unfavourable terms of trade. Their export income is much smaller than the import payments they make for high value imports resulting in large foreign debt levels.  
*   **Promotes Domestic Growth:** All round industrial development will occur in the country since it cannot depend on foreign industries.  

---

## 3. Trade Policy
*   **Definition:** Trade policy refers to the regulations and policies that state how a country carries out international trade with other foreign countries. It is also referred to as commercial policy.  

### Key Components
*   It consists of tariffs on imported goods, quotas, export constraints and restrictions on the domestic operations of foreign companies.  
*   Subsidies may also be provided to domestic industries to enable them to compete with foreign industries.  
*   A major component of trade policy are trade barriers consisting of tariffs and non-tariff barriers.

# Trade Barriers: Tariffs and Non-Tariff Barriers (NTBs)

## I. Tariffs

### Definition and Purpose
*   A tariff is defined as a tax that one country imposes on goods and services imported from another country.  
*   The primary use of a tariff is to restrict imports.  
*   They work by increasing the price of foreign goods, which makes them less appealing to domestic consumers.  
*   The dual intent of tariffs is to discourage the consumption of foreign goods while simultaneously encouraging the production and consumption of domestically produced substitutes.  

### Types of Tariffs
Governments can employ several different types of tariffs:  
*   **Specific tariffs:** A fixed tariff amount applied to a single unit of an imported or exported good.  
*   **Ad valorem tariffs:** A tariff levied as a percentage of the imported good's overall value.  
*   **Countervailing tariffs:** Tariffs specifically levied on imported commodities that receive heavy subsidies from their foreign governments.  
*   **Anti-dumping tariffs:** Tariffs imposed on foreign goods that are being sold domestically at a price lower than their actual cost of production.  

### Advantages of Tariffs
*   **Revenue Generation:** They serve as a direct source of revenue for the government.  
*   **Infant Industry Protection:** Developing industries can use initial tariff protection to grow and effectively compete with already developed foreign industries.  
*   **Promote Domestic Goods:** By artificially raising the price of foreign goods, domestic alternatives appear much more attractive to consumers.  
*   **Targeted Support:** Governments can utilize tariffs to benefit specific, targeted domestic industries.  
*   **Economic Protection:** They help protect domestic companies and local jobs from foreign competition.  

### Disadvantages of Tariffs
*   **Market Distortion:** Tariffs create artificial distortions in global trade.  
*   **Harm to Consumers:** A lack of foreign competition generally pushes prices up, which hurts domestic consumers.  
*   **Reduced Innovation:** Shielded from global competition, domestic industries can become less efficient and less innovative.  
*   **Decreased Trade:** They inevitably lead to a fall in the overall volume of international trade.  
*   **Domestic Tension:** Favoring specific industries with tariffs can generate economic tensions among other domestic sectors.  
*   **Trade Wars:** Using tariffs to pressure rival nations can easily devolve into an unproductive cycle of retaliation, known as a trade war.  

---

## II. Non-Tariff Barriers (NTBs)

### Definition and Nature
*   A non-tariff barrier is a method of restricting trade using mechanisms other than a traditional tariff.  
*   While conventional tariffs are considered "visible" trade barriers, NTBs act as hidden or invisible barriers to trade.  
*   In recent years, NTBs have actually grown in prominence compared to conventional tariffs.  
*   The overarching effect of NTBs is identical to tariffs: they restrict and distort trade, cause a misallocation of global resources, and ultimately reduce global welfare.  

### Types of Non-Tariff Barriers
NTBs encompass direct restrictions, monetary controls, and various administrative regulations:  
*   **Quotas:** Quantitative limits designed to restrict the size of imports or exports. Quotas are often considered more severe than tariffs because they physically cap the number of products allowed into a country. Mandatory import quotas are much more common globally than export quotas.  
*   **Voluntary Export Restraints:** An arrangement where exporting countries are asked to voluntarily limit their own exports.  
*   **Licenses:** Governments use trade licenses to restrict imported goods to specific, approved businesses; only those with a license can import goods that are otherwise restricted.  
*   **Monetary Restrictions:** A country may implement foreign exchange controls to severely limit import volume. The government can actively deny importers access to the foreign exchange needed to buy specific imports or purchase from specific countries. This is especially widespread in poorer nations facing foreign exchange shortages.  
*   **Administrative Regulations:** Includes customs bans on products deemed health or safety hazards. Examples include restrictions on food, fruit, or toys that potentially endanger the public.  
*   **Technical Regulations:** Mandates regarding specific labeling and packaging standards.  

### Advantages of NTBs
*   **Industrial Development:** NTBs give domestic industries the room needed to grow, achieve economies of scale, and become competitive internationally.  
*   **Strategic Demand Shift:** A decline in imports forcibly diverts consumer demand toward domestic products, supporting strategic development.  
*   **Job Creation:** To meet increased demand, domestic firms invest in capital goods and hire more local workers.  
*   **Economic Growth:** Ultimately, this creates more income and broader growth within the domestic economy.  

### Disadvantages of NTBs
*   **No Government Revenue:** Unlike tariffs (which act as a tax), NTBs do not generate any extra income for the government.  
*   **Hinders the Free Market:** They prevent the free market from functioning properly, disrupting the ideal scenario where countries specialize based on comparative advantage to maximize global benefits.  
*   **Increased Business Costs:** NTBs raise operating costs because companies must fulfill complex administrative requirements, such as product standardization.  
*   **Red Tape:** Companies are forced to navigate highly complicated customs procedures.  
*   **Unfair Competition:** They naturally put foreign exporters at a distinct disadvantage compared to domestic companies.
